But the problem with Bodhidharma is that he cannot accept things simply as
they are. He says:
HERE, THE BATHING OF MONKS DOESN'T REFER TO THE WASHING OF
ANYTHING TANGIBLE.
How do you wash anything intangible? A thing that is not tangible is not visible
either. Only tangible things have to be washed. Your body can be given a shower
but not your soul. Your clothes can be cleaned but not your being. But that does
not mean that you have to use dirty clothes, that you have to remain dirty in
your body.
Buddha was very aesthetic in comparison to Mahavira, his contemporary, and
that's why he has more grace than Mahavira. Mahavira has a very strong
personality but he's not graceful ...a personality of a wrestler, but not the
individuality of a lotus flower.
It is not accidental that Gautam Buddha has become synonymous with the lotus
flower. It is so fragile and so beautiful and so graceful that no other flower on the
earth even comes close to it. He wanted his monks to be sensitive, aesthetic,
clean, and naturally the only way was to tell the people that if you help these
poor monks with food, with a bath, with medicine, with clothes you will be
getting great merit in the other world. This was simply a pragmatic affair.
