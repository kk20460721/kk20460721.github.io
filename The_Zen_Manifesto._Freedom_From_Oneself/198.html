One afternoon down in Podunk, Alabama, at the Abe Lincoln Elementary School, Miss Velcro, the
black teacher, is giving an English lesson.
”Now, class,” says Miss Velcro, ”today we are going to write some poetry. And for our theme, we are
going to go back to our black roots in Africa. I want you to write a poem using that famous African
town, Timbuktu.”
For the next ten minutes, everybody writes. And at the end of the class, Miss Velcro says, ”Okay,
children, before we go home, would anyone like to read out their poem to the class?”
Two boys, Little Black George and Little Black Washington, raise their hands enthusiastically.
”Good,” says Miss Velcro. ”George, you read yours ﬁrst.”
George stands up and says proudly:
”Far away in a distant land,
Out across the burning sand,
Black men on camels march two by two,
On their way to Timbuktu!”
The classroom cheers loudly, and then Miss Velcro says, ”That was very good, George. Now let us
hear your poem, Washington.”
